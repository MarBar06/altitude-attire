The Hidden Cost of Flight

Flying is not neutral for the body

Only those who live in the sky know what it truly feels like

Flying does more than make you tired it places real physiological stress on the body. When you're flying, factors such as cabin pressure, low humidity, prolonged sitting or standing, and disrupted schedules may contribute to physical discomfort and fatigue.

Key Impacts of Flying

Circulation Stress

Prolonged sitting or standing at altitude reduces blood flow, contributing to swelling, heaviness, and discomfort, especially in the legs and feet.

Low Cabin Humidity

Cabin humidity drops to 10–20%, which may increase fluid loss, drying the skin, and impairing circulation and focus.

Digestive Discomfort

Cabin pressure and reduced oxygen slow digestion, increasing bloating, gas, and gastrointestinal discomfort.

Immune System Suppression

Low humidity, disrupted sleep, and repeated exposure to enclosed environments can temporarily weaken immune response.

Fatigue & Burnout

Frequent altitude changes, physical demands, and long duty hours drain energy reserves faster than normal daily life.

Sleep Disruption

Crossing time zones confuses your circadian rhythm—awake when you should sleep, exhausted when you need to perform.

This is the hidden cost of life in the sky
